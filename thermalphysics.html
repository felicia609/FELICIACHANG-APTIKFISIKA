Thermal Physics

Brownian/Molecular Movement

Brownian motion refers to the random, erratic movement of particles suspended in a fluid (liquid or gas), caused by constant collisions with molecules in the surrounding medium. This supports the kinetic theory of matter, where particles are always in motion.

Conversion of Temperature Scales

  • Celsius to Kelvin: K = °C + 273.15
  • Kelvin to Celsius: °C = K - 273.15
  • Celsius to Fahrenheit: °F = (°C × 9/5) + 32
  • Fahrenheit to Celsius: °C = (°F - 32) × 5/9

Why Kelvin is the SI Unit

Kelvin is the SI unit for temperature because it starts at absolute zero (0 K), the point where all molecular motion stops. This makes it a direct and uniform measure of thermal energy.

Thermal Expansion in Solids and Liquids

Thermal expansion is the increase in size or volume of a substance when heated:

  • Solids: Expand in a uniform manner; can be quantified using a linear expansion coefficient.
  • Liquids: Expand more than solids due to weaker intermolecular forces.

Anomalous Expansion of Water

Water expands when cooled from 4°C to 0°C, unlike most substances that contract. This is why ice is less dense than liquid water and floats.
